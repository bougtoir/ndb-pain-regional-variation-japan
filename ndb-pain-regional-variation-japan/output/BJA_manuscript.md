## Abstract

**Background:** Cultural and regional factors influence pain expression and analgesic consumption. In Japan, the Tohoku region is traditionally perceived as having a stoic, pain-enduring population. We tested the hypothesis that Tohoku prefectures would demonstrate lower perioperative analgesic prescribing per surgical procedure compared with other regions.

**Methods:** This ecological study used the 10th edition of Japan's National Database of Health Insurance Claims (NDB) Open Data, covering all insurance-reimbursed healthcare from April 2023 to March 2024. Prefecture-level inpatient analgesic prescription quantities (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs [NSAIDs], opioid alkaloids, and synthetic opioids) were divided by inpatient surgical procedure counts to derive an analgesic-per-surgery index for each of the 47 prefectures. The nine standard regional blocks were compared using Kruskal-Wallis and Mann-Whitney U tests.

**Results:** Across 7,903,515 inpatient surgical procedures and 274,579,851 analgesic prescription units nationally, substantial regional variation was observed (Kruskal-Wallis H=34.10, P<0.001). Contrary to the stoicism hypothesis, Tohoku ranked 7th of 9 regions with a mean analgesic-per-surgery index of 39.97 (SD 3.53) versus the national mean of 35.78, representing 11.7% higher use (Mann-Whitney U=190, P=0.031; Cohen's d=0.87). This pattern was consistent across all drug classes: NSAIDs (P=0.044), opioid alkaloids (P=0.003), and synthetic opioids (P=0.001). A north-south gradient was apparent, with the highest use in Hokkaido (46.12) and Kyushu-Okinawa (42.26), and the lowest in Tokai (27.62) and Kinki (30.02).

**Conclusions:** Perioperative analgesic prescribing in Japan's Tohoku region was significantly higher, not lower, than the national average. Perceived regional stoicism does not translate into reduced analgesic consumption at the ecological level. These findings highlight the importance of evidence-based assessment over cultural stereotypes in perioperative pain management.

## Introduction

Pain perception and expression are shaped by cultural, social, and regional factors.^1,2^ International studies have demonstrated substantial cross-national variation in postoperative pain management, with culture influencing both patient reporting behaviour and clinician prescribing patterns.^3,4^ Within countries, regional variation in analgesic prescribing has been well documented, particularly for opioids in the United States and Europe.^5,6^

Japan presents a unique context for studying regional variation in pain management. Despite operating within a uniform national health insurance system that eliminates financial barriers to access, Japan has documented regional differences in various healthcare utilisation metrics.^7^ The Tohoku region (northeastern Honshu, comprising Aomori, Iwate, Miyagi, Akita, Yamagata, and Fukushima prefectures) is culturally characterised as having a particularly stoic population, with residents commonly described as *gaman-zuyoi* (patient/enduring).^8^ A 2017 nationwide survey by Pfizer Japan found that the proportion of respondents reporting that they endure pain varied from 48.7% to 81.6% across prefectures, with northern prefectures generally scoring higher.^9^

Whether this perceived stoicism translates into measurable differences in perioperative analgesic consumption has not been formally tested. Recent studies have begun to characterise regional variation in analgesic use in Japan. Matsuoka and colleagues^10^ demonstrated significant prefectural variation in opioid prescribing for cancer pain using National Database (NDB) claims data, with oxycodone injection use ranging from 4.0% in Shikoku to 16.4% in Tokai. Shoji and colleagues^11^ reported regional differences in multimodal analgesia adoption following total knee arthroplasty.

Japan's NDB Open Data provides a unique opportunity to conduct comprehensive ecological analyses of analgesic prescribing patterns. The NDB captures virtually all insurance-reimbursed healthcare claims in Japan (approximately 2.1 billion claims annually), and the aggregated open data are freely available without institutional access requirements.^12^

The primary objective of this study was to test whether prefectures in the Tohoku region demonstrated lower perioperative analgesic prescribing per surgical procedure compared with other regions, as would be predicted by the cultural stoicism hypothesis. The secondary objectives were to characterise the overall pattern of regional variation and to examine whether patterns differed by analgesic class (NSAIDs, opioid alkaloids, and synthetic opioids).

## Methods

### Study design and reporting

This ecological study analysed prefecture-level aggregate data from the NDB Open Data. The study is reported following the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology (STROBE) guidelines for cross-sectional studies^13^ and the REporting of studies Conducted using Observational Routinely-collected health Data (RECORD) extension.^14^ As the study used only publicly available aggregate data with no individual-level information, ethical approval was not required.

### Data source

The 10th edition of the NDB Open Data (published by the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare [MHLW]) was used, covering healthcare claims from April 2023 to March 2024.^12^ The NDB captures claims from all insurers within Japan's universal health coverage system, encompassing approximately 125 million insured individuals. Aggregate data are published at the prefecture level with suppression of cells containing fewer than 10 events (for procedure data) or 1,000 units (for prescription data) to prevent re-identification.

### Outcome: analgesic prescription quantity

Inpatient prescription data were extracted from the file "内服薬 入院 都道府県別薬効分類別数量" (Inpatient oral medications: prescription quantity by prefecture and drug efficacy classification). Three analgesic drug classes were identified using the Japanese drug efficacy classification system:

- **Class 114**: Antipyretic analgesics and anti-inflammatory drugs (解熱鎮痛消炎剤), encompassing NSAIDs and acetaminophen
- **Class 811**: Opium alkaloid narcotics (あへんアルカロイド系麻薬), including morphine, oxycodone, and codeine
- **Class 821**: Synthetic narcotics (合成麻薬), including fentanyl, pethidine, and tapentadol

The total analgesic prescription quantity for each prefecture was calculated as the sum across all three classes of all individual drug items within that class.

### Exposure: surgical procedure volume

Inpatient surgical procedure data were extracted from the file "K手術 款別都道府県別算定回数" (K Surgery: procedure counts by section and prefecture). The total surgical procedure count for each prefecture was calculated as the sum of all procedure items across all surgical sections (Section K of the Japanese medical fee schedule). Suppressed cells (indicated by "–") were treated as zero.

### Analgesic-per-surgery index

The primary metric was calculated for each prefecture as:

> Analgesic-per-surgery index = Total analgesic prescription quantity (units) / Total inpatient surgical procedure count

This index represents the average number of analgesic prescription units (tablets, capsules, or millilitres) prescribed per inpatient surgical procedure within each prefecture.

### Regional classification

Prefectures were grouped into nine standard regional blocks used by the Japanese government: Hokkaido (n=1), Tohoku (n=6: Aomori, Iwate, Miyagi, Akita, Yamagata, Fukushima), Kanto (n=7), Hokuriku-Koshinetsu (n=6), Tokai (n=4), Kinki (n=6), Chugoku (n=5), Shikoku (n=4), and Kyushu-Okinawa (n=8).

### Statistical analysis

Descriptive statistics (mean, standard deviation [SD], median, range) were calculated for each prefecture and regional block. The primary comparison was between Tohoku (n=6 prefectures) and the rest of Japan (n=41 prefectures). Given the small sample size and non-normal distribution, the Mann-Whitney U test was used for the primary comparison, with both two-sided and one-sided (Tohoku < Rest) tests. The independent t-test was also reported. Cohen's d was calculated as the effect size, with 95% confidence intervals (CI) for the mean difference. The Kruskal-Wallis test was used for the overall comparison across all nine regional blocks. Subgroup analyses were conducted for each analgesic class separately. Statistical significance was set at P<0.05 (two-sided). All analyses were performed using Python 3.11 with pandas 2.0, NumPy 1.24, SciPy 1.11, and matplotlib 3.8.

## Results

### National overview

During the study period (April 2023 to March 2024), the NDB recorded 7,903,515 inpatient surgical procedures and 274,579,851 analgesic prescription units (oral medications) across Japan's 47 prefectures. Of the total analgesic quantity, NSAIDs and acetaminophen (Class 114) accounted for 95.6% (262,451,388 units), opioid alkaloids (Class 811) for 4.1% (11,368,144 units), and synthetic opioids (Class 821) for 0.3% (760,319 units).

The national mean analgesic-per-surgery index was 35.78 (SD 5.56), with substantial variation ranging from 25.20 (Gifu) to 49.75 (Kagoshima), a 1.97-fold difference between the lowest and highest prefectures (Table 1).

### Regional block comparison

Significant variation was observed across the nine regional blocks (Kruskal-Wallis H=34.10, P<0.001; Table 2, Fig. 1). A broad geographical gradient was apparent: Tokai (27.62, SD 2.18) and Kinki (30.02, SD 2.03) in central-western Japan had the lowest mean analgesic-per-surgery indices, while Hokkaido (46.12) and Kyushu-Okinawa (42.26, SD 4.33) in the northern and southern peripheries had the highest.

### Primary analysis: Tohoku versus rest of Japan

Contrary to the stoicism hypothesis, the Tohoku region demonstrated significantly *higher* analgesic prescribing per surgery compared with the rest of Japan (Table 3). The mean analgesic-per-surgery index was 39.97 (SD 3.53) in Tohoku versus 35.17 (SD 5.71) in the rest of Japan, a difference of 4.80 units (95% CI: 1.48 to 8.12). This difference was statistically significant by the Mann-Whitney U test (U=190, P=0.031) and borderline by the t-test (t=1.99, P=0.053). The effect size was large (Cohen's d=0.87). The one-sided test for lower Tohoku use was non-significant (P=0.986), effectively excluding the stoicism hypothesis at this ecological level.

Tohoku ranked 7th of 9 regions (with 1 being the lowest), with mean analgesic use 11.7% above the national average. Within Tohoku, all six prefectures ranked in the upper half nationally: Akita (44.51, rank 44/47), Aomori (42.99, rank 41/47), Iwate (40.89, rank 38/47), Yamagata (39.16, rank 33/47), Miyagi (37.10, rank 30/47), and Fukushima (35.18, rank 25/47).

### Drug class subgroup analysis

The pattern of higher Tohoku analgesic use was consistent across all three drug classes (Fig. 2):

- **NSAIDs/acetaminophen (Class 114):** Tohoku mean 37.90 (SD 2.99) versus rest 33.66 (SD 5.49); Mann-Whitney U=186, P=0.044.
- **Opioid alkaloids (Class 811):** Tohoku mean 1.91 (SD 0.35) versus rest 1.43 (SD 0.27); Mann-Whitney U=211, P=0.003.
- **Synthetic opioids (Class 821):** Tohoku mean 0.16 (SD 0.06) versus rest 0.09 (SD 0.04); Mann-Whitney U=217, P=0.001.

The opioid findings are particularly notable: Tohoku prefectures prescribed 34% more opioid alkaloids and 86% more synthetic opioids per surgery compared with the rest of Japan, with these differences being highly significant (P=0.003 and P=0.001, respectively).

## Discussion

### Principal findings

This nationwide ecological study using Japan's NDB Open Data found that perioperative analgesic prescribing in the Tohoku region was significantly higher, not lower, than in the rest of Japan. The Tohoku region ranked 7th of 9 regional blocks, with a mean analgesic-per-surgery index 11.7% above the national average and a large effect size (Cohen's d=0.87). This pattern was consistent across NSAIDs, opioid alkaloids, and synthetic opioids. These findings directly contradict the hypothesis that the perceived cultural stoicism of Tohoku residents would manifest as lower perioperative analgesic consumption.

### Comparison with existing evidence

Our findings align with, and extend, previous work on regional variation in analgesic prescribing in Japan. Matsuoka and colleagues^10^ reported substantial prefectural variation in cancer pain opioid prescribing using NDB data, though their focus was on cancer rather than surgical patients. The broad geographical gradient we observed—with higher use in the northern and southern peripheries and lower use in central Japan—parallels patterns reported in regional healthcare utilisation studies.^7^

The 2017 Pfizer Japan survey^9^ found that northern prefectures reported higher rates of self-described pain endurance. Our data suggest that this self-reported stoicism does not suppress perioperative analgesic prescribing—and may paradoxically coexist with higher prescribing. Several non-mutually exclusive explanations merit consideration.

### Possible explanations

First, the ecological fallacy must be acknowledged: individual-level pain endurance may not aggregate into prefecture-level prescribing differences because prescribing is primarily determined by clinician behaviour, institutional protocols, and drug availability rather than patient stoicism alone.^15^ Patients who endure pain silently may still receive standardised analgesic protocols.

Second, the geographical gradient may reflect differences in surgical case mix. Northern prefectures (Tohoku, Hokkaido) have older populations with higher rates of musculoskeletal and oncological surgery, which typically require more intensive analgesia.^16^ Central urban prefectures may have a higher proportion of minimally invasive and ambulatory procedures. The NDB Open Data do not allow case-mix adjustment at the aggregate level, limiting our ability to disentangle this confounding.

Third, regional variation in prescribing culture and practice patterns among clinicians may be the dominant driver. Japanese anaesthesia practice is not fully standardised nationally, and regional academic networks (ikyoku system) can create persistent prescribing clusters.^17^ The higher opioid use in Tohoku may reflect specific institutional champions or training programmes that promote multimodal opioid-inclusive analgesia.

Fourth, the higher use in peripheral regions may partly reflect longer hospital stays. Japan has among the longest average lengths of hospital stay globally, and rural hospitals tend to have even longer stays.^18^ Longer inpatient stays mechanically increase cumulative analgesic prescribing per surgical admission.

### Implications

Our findings have several implications for perioperative practice and research. First, cultural stereotypes about regional pain tolerance should not influence clinical analgesic prescribing. The assumption that Tohoku patients require less analgesia because they are "stoic" is not supported by these data and could lead to systematic under-treatment if acted upon.

Second, the nearly two-fold variation in analgesic-per-surgery index across prefectures (25.20 to 49.75) suggests substantial scope for standardisation. Whether the optimal level is closer to the lower end (potentially indicating adequate pain control with less medication burden) or the higher end (potentially indicating more comprehensive pain management) requires patient-level studies with pain outcome data.

Third, the particularly large regional variation in opioid prescribing (up to 86% higher in Tohoku for synthetic opioids) warrants attention in the context of Japan's evolving opioid policies. While Japan has historically had very low opioid consumption compared with Western countries,^19^ regional disparities in access to appropriate opioid analgesia may represent an equity issue.

### Strengths and limitations

This study has several strengths. The NDB captures virtually all insurance-reimbursed healthcare in Japan, providing near-complete population coverage without selection bias. The data are recent (2023-2024) and represent the latest available national claims. The analgesic-per-surgery index is an intuitive metric that enables direct regional comparison.

Several limitations must be acknowledged. First, as an ecological study, inferences about individual-level associations are not possible. The analgesic-per-surgery index divides total analgesic prescriptions by total surgeries within each prefecture, but these are not linked at the individual level—the analgesics may include prescriptions for non-surgical patients and the surgeries may include procedures not requiring systemic analgesia. Second, the NDB Open Data provide only oral (内服薬) prescription data; parenteral analgesics (intravenous opioids, epidural infusions, nerve blocks) are captured in separate procedure codes and could not be linked to specific drug quantities. This systematically underestimates total analgesic exposure, particularly for the immediate postoperative period when parenteral routes predominate. Third, the ecological unit is the patient's insurance prefecture, which may differ from the treatment prefecture for patients who travel for surgery, potentially introducing misclassification. Fourth, we could not adjust for case mix (surgical complexity, patient age distribution, comorbidities), length of stay, or institutional factors. Fifth, suppressed cells in the NDB (below 1,000 units for drugs or 10 for procedures) were treated as zero, which may slightly underestimate values in smaller prefectures. Sixth, the analysis is limited to a single fiscal year, preventing assessment of temporal trends.

### Future directions

Individual-level analyses using NDB Special Extract data or DPC (Diagnosis Procedure Combination) claims would allow case-mix adjustment and procedure-specific analgesic quantification. Linkage with patient-reported pain outcomes (e.g., through a prospective multicentre study) would enable the critical distinction between prescribing patterns and actual pain control. Such studies should incorporate validated measures of pain attitudes and endurance to directly test the cultural stoicism hypothesis at the individual level.

## Conclusions

This nationwide ecological study found that perioperative analgesic prescribing in Japan's Tohoku region was significantly higher, not lower, than the national average, with large and consistent effects across NSAIDs and opioids. The perceived cultural stoicism of Tohoku residents does not translate into reduced analgesic consumption at the prefecture level. Nearly two-fold variation across prefectures highlights the need for standardisation of perioperative pain management. Cultural stereotypes should not substitute for evidence-based, individualised analgesic prescribing.
